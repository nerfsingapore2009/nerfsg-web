# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Singaporeans aged 12-40 who play or are curious about foam dart games. Two sub-groups:

- **Regulars**: existing community members who check schedules, browse past games, and use the NerfSG Hub app. They scan fast, trust the site, want the next date and location.
- **Newcomers**: people who saw a TikTok, heard from a friend, or got tagged in a photo. They are deciding whether to show up. They need to see that it looks fun, safe, and accessible before they commit.

Context: mobile-first. Most visitors arrive via a shared link from Telegram or Instagram. They are curious but low-commitment; the site has roughly 30 seconds to earn a second scroll.

## Product Purpose

NerfSG is the public face of Singapore's foam dart community. The site exists to convert curiosity into attendance. Success looks like: a newcomer arrives, sees real people having real fun, finds the next game, and joins the Telegram or downloads the app.

Secondary: it serves as a reference hub (rules, guides, past games, game modes) that regulars link to and return to.

## Brand Personality

Fun · Energetic · Welcoming

Voice: direct, conversational, never corporate. Sounds like the most enthusiastic person at the game briefing. Uses plain English. Never uses the word "community" in the SaaS sense. Says "come play" not "join our ecosystem."

## Anti-references

- **Generic Discord-tier hobby sites** (cheap dark themes, wall-of-text rules, no photos, looks unmaintained). The site must feel actively curated, not just running.
- **SaaS startup landing pages** (feature bullets, gradient accent CTAs, "Supercharge your hobby" copy, stock icons, hero-metric stat blocks). This is a people-and-sport brand, not a product launch.
- **Corporate esports aesthetics** (overly tactical, intimidating, designed for pros). NerfSG is explicitly for all skill levels; the design must not signal "you need to be good to belong."
- **NERF toy brand visual language** (primary colors, cartoon energy, age 8-12 target). The audience is adult; the aesthetic should be mature action-sport, not toy marketing.

## Design Principles

1. **Show the game, not the brand.** Real field photos and authentic player moments carry more weight than any logo treatment or abstract graphic. If a section has no real image, make the typography and layout do the work instead of reaching for illustration or placeholder patterns.
2. **Anyone can show up.** Hierarchy, copy, and CTAs should make attending a first game feel achievable and low-stakes, not intimidating. Surface the "come join" invitation before the rules and logistics.
3. **Earned energy, not performed hype.** Motion, bold type, and color should reflect actual game intensity, not inflate it. One strong red accent does more than five gradient overlays.
4. **Authentic over polished.** Real names, real dates, real player handles in credits. No buzzwords, no "cutting-edge foam dart experience." Specificity builds trust; vagueness kills it.
5. **Mobile is the primary surface.** Every layout decision starts at 375px and earns its desktop expansion. Tap targets, readable text at arm's length, no horizontal overflow.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Target WCAG 2.1 AA as the floor. Body text contrast ≥ 4.5:1 against background at all times.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`: all animations must have a static fallback.
- All interactive elements need visible focus states (already present via `focus-visible` throughout).
- Alt text on all photos, especially gallery and hero images where the image carries content meaning.
